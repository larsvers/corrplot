Data has the tendency to come in rows for observations and columns for the variables, measures, metrics or whatever you might want to call it. Whenever I meet a new dataset, I am ambling along its columns to get an understanding of it — to profile it. Quite literally, I move from column to column and dedicate a good moment to get to know a little — typically running frequencies, calculate normal values and check for missings.

While this first approach is more of a personal conversation under four eyes I try to get more social in the second approach: understanding relations between variables. With numerical data one would typically bring a correlation matrix along, which gives us an overview of associations across all numeric variables in a dataset.

Recently, I've done so analysing 1,600 wines and their physiochemical properties, making up a wine's liquid composition, in order to build a model aimed to predict a wine's quality. Properties like Alcohol, sugars, chlorides (salt), pH Value, acids and more.

In fact, there's a whole little visual story about these efforts over here, and originally this thingy here was supposed to be part of that story, but it didn't quite fit, so it gets its very own little stage.

Be that as may, the wine quality model we build over there is a Logistic Regression — a general linear model, which comes with the benefit of being relatively simple to explain becasue all it does is modelling correlations between — in our case — numeric variables. To get an inital overview, one would usually use a correlation matrix like so:

I like correlation matrixes. They do the job. Seeing a correlation matrix for the first time, you might have to orient yourself a little, but once that's done you quickly understand how to read it. For the sake of completeness: all your variables are in the rows and the columns and the circles encode the correlation magnitude with their size and possibly their colour lightness and the correlation direction with hue.

This is all you need to know to efficiently look up each correlation of interest like for example the correlation between a wine's pH values and Citric Acid,

which seems to be negative, indicated by the orange tint, and quite notable — in this case it's -0.54.

Or between alcohol and density, also negative, telling us that the higher the alcohol → the lower the density.

Or between the all important wine quality and alcohol, which — maybe unsurpsingly — is positive. The higher the alcohol → the higher the quality (we can sense the limits of correlations in reality here as there's probably a tipping point a simple correlation won't capture).

As nice and efficient as this all is, it carries some slack, we can prune away; like for example the diagonal of auto-correlations. It is sort of pretty obvious that each variable is perfectly positively correlated with itself. So off we go.

There are great visualisation packages out there some of which use this space to show for example the density distribution of that variable, but to keep it minimal we're just removing it here.

But there's more to cut. In fact, more than half of the matrix can go, as the bottom right grid is just a reflection of the upper left grid.

This is it, I guess. To get a full overview, we can't really cut away anything else. We can tilt it into a correlation pyramid for different aesthetics,

...but that's a matter of taste.

Now, the reason I started climbing down into the correlation matrix basement to start with, was a faint call from the 3rd dimension. Whenever I look at correlation matrixes, my inner eye doesn't only see circles encoding the correlation strength and direction but bars, or cylinders pointing up or down. We can't see them as we're looking straight down onto its flat survace, but someone needed to free them I thought. It turns out the 3rd dimension isn't all too helpful when you have a full matrix — as you can't see the tree for the forest, as it were. Too busy.

BUT our particular interest in the data (full story over here, I won't mention it again, thank you very much) is on correlation of all variables with just a single variable: the wine quality!

So, focusising in on quality...
